02 — Layers of analysis

What the game is, and where it comes from

Ontological analysis

The game is built from four connected modules: farmland, feedlot, restaurant, and corporate headquarters. The farmland module handles soy cultivation and cattle raising, including whether to use genetically modified feed and whether to clear rainforest for more pasture. The feedlot module handles fattening and slaughtering cattle, including the option to feed cattle to other cattle, a practice linked to mad cow disease. The restaurant module handles staffing and food production. The corporate headquarters module handles advertising, public relations, and bribery of officials and nutritionists.

Underneath these four modules, the same core variables move throughout the game: money, a public backlash meter called detractors, and physical resources including land, cattle, and soy. The game ends if debt crosses a set threshold. There is no win condition. Playing ethically causes immediate bankruptcy, while playing to survive forces deforestation, poor labor conditions, and cover-ups.

Historical and contextual analysis

Released in Italian at the end of 2005 and in English shortly after, during the peak of Flash browser gaming, when games could reach millions of players for free through portals such as Newgrounds and Miniclip. The project responds directly to the alter-globalization movement of the early 2000s: Naomi Klein's No Logo, Fast Food Nation, Super Size Me, and real mad cow disease scares were all recent public memory. Pedercini has cited Jeremy Rifkin's Beyond Beef as a direct influence.

Pedercini founded Molleindustria in 2003. The game also directly answers a claim by Will Wright, creator of SimCity, that simulation games are neutral entertainment — Pedercini argues every simulation encodes its designer's assumptions, whether intended or not.

Visual and aesthetic analysis

Unlike an article or documentary, the game does not describe its argument, it makes the player perform it.

The argument is not stated, it is enacted through the player's own actions inside the system.

This is Ian Bogost's procedural rhetoric: persuasion through the rules and processes of a system rather than through images or text. With no win condition, the player keeps returning to the same decisions under rising pressure, understanding the message gradually through repetition rather than reading it once.

The game appropriates McDonald's own branding directly — color scheme, mascot, a parody jingle — reinforcing that naming a real company is more rhetorically effective than a fictional stand-in.

03 — Relational diagrams

How is the system structured?

Three diagrams model the game from different angles.

Who and what is involved

The player manages four modules. Material flows sequentially from Farmland to Feedlot to Restaurant. Corporate HQ separately funds relationships with Investors, Officials, and Nutritionists, and advertises to the Public. Farmland, Feedlot, and Restaurant each independently contribute to public backlash, and backlash and debt both feed back to threaten investor confidence, closing a loop rather than forming a strict hierarchy.

How a single round actually runs

Each round, the player manages all four modules in parallel through a fork and join. Finances and detractors update, then a decision checks whether an end condition has been met. Most rounds answer no and simply continue — the diagram documents the mechanism only, it does not claim the game is unwinnable.

Where the room to maneuver runs out

A rhetorical companion to the diagram above: investor demand and public tolerance converge toward each other across the four modules, narrowing the safe zone to zero regardless of the path taken.

04 — Methodology

Why build it this way?

Choosing a game at all was already a deliberate move, before any choice of software. Games let you represent a system by making the player operate it, rather than describing it from outside.

Software approach

Flash meant free in-browser play with no download, reaching a broad casual audience. Released under a Creative Commons NonCommercial license, the frictionless, free distribution was itself political: corporate critique circulating outside corporate channels.

Data uses and provenance

Jeremy Rifkin's Beyond Beef sources Farmland and Feedlot; Naomi Klein's No Logo sources Corporate HQ specifically. The four-module structure traces to two pieces of published critique, not first-hand corporate research.

Theoretical methods

Bogost's Persuasive Games (2007) postdates the game by a year — procedural rhetoric is a retrospective lens, not Pedercini's own framework at the time. His stated theory was closer to media-activism, citing Ted Friedman's point that it is easier to imagine a videogame based on Marx's Capital than a movie based on it: games represent systems, film represents stories.

05 — Rhetorical analysis

What is the game arguing, and how?

The game never states that industrial food production is exploitative. It makes ethical choices consistently more expensive than unethical ones, letting the player reach that conclusion through play — persuasion by doing rather than telling.

This is not uncontested: the game's framing as play may blunt its stakes; optimizing an unethical system can feel satisfying in a way that risks the player enjoying exploitation rather than condemning it; politically-coded games tend to reach players already sympathetic. Pedercini's position is that games are never neutral — making the politics explicit only refuses to disguise what mainstream games already encode by default.

06 — Wider practice

Where does this sit in Molleindustria's practice?

2003

Molleindustria founded to make "radical games against the dictatorship of entertainment." Earlier work — Tamatipico, Tuboflex, Queer Power — stays smaller and Italian-labor-focused.

2005

The McDonald's Videogame — a shift toward a globally recognizable target and a mass casual-games audience.

2009

Every Day the Same Dream moves toward more existential, less overtly polemical territory.

2011

Phone Story applies the same anti-advergame logic to smartphone manufacturing, until Apple removes it from the App Store.

Ongoing

Pedercini continues the practice through teaching, running an experimental game design course at Carnegie Mellon University.
