Layers of Analysis

1. Ontological Analysis

The game is built from four connected modules: farmland, feedlot, restaurant, and corporate headquarters. The farmland module handles soy cultivation and cattle raising, including whether to use genetically modified feed and whether to clear rainforest for more pasture. The feedlot module handles fattening and slaughtering cattle, including the option to feed cattle to other cattle, a practice linked to mad cow disease. The restaurant module handles staffing and food production. The corporate headquarters module handles advertising, public relations, and bribery of officials and nutritionists.

Underneath these four modules, the same core variables move throughout the game: money, a public backlash meter called detractors, and physical resources including land, cattle, and soy. The game ends if debt crosses a set threshold. There is no win condition. Playing ethically causes immediate bankruptcy, while playing to survive forces deforestation, poor labor conditions, and cover-ups. The game continues indefinitely, with investor demands escalating until either the environment collapses or public backlash becomes unmanageable.

Structurally, the game uses the tycoon and business-simulation genre, the same genre as SimCity and RollerCoaster Tycoon.

2. Historical and Contextual Analysis

The game was released in Italian at the end of 2005 and in English shortly after, during the peak of Flash browser game popularity, when games could reach millions of players for free through portals such as Newgrounds and Miniclip.

The project responds directly to the alter-globalization movement of the early 2000s. Naomi Klein's No Logo had already brought anti-corporate branding critique into the mainstream. Fast Food Nation and Super Size Me had put the fast food supply chain under public scrutiny. Real mad cow disease scares were recent public memory. Pedercini has cited Jeremy Rifkin's Beyond Beef as a direct influence on the game.

Pedercini founded Molleindustria in 2003 as an activist game-making platform. The McDonald's Videogame followed earlier, smaller political games about Italian labor rights, meaning it was a continuation of an existing practice rather than a first attempt.

The game also directly responds to a claim by Will Wright, creator of SimCity, that simulation games are neutral entertainment with no political content. Pedercini disagreed, arguing that every simulation game encodes its designer's assumptions about how the world works, whether the designer intends it or not. The McDonald's Videogame was built to make that political content explicit rather than hidden.

The intended audience was the general public browsing Flash portals for casual entertainment, not an audience already seeking political games. The tycoon-sim genre was chosen because it reads as neutral entertainment, allowing the critique to reach players before they realize the game is making an argument. The real McDonald's name was used deliberately, rather than a fictional stand-in, following the logic that criticizing a real, named target is more effective than a vague or generalized criticism.

3. Visual and Aesthetic Analysis

Unlike an article, PDF, or documentary, the McDonald's Videogame does not simply describe its argument to the reader, it makes the player perform it. A text can tell you that running a fast food company forces trade-offs between profit and ethics, but the game requires you to actually make those trade-offs yourself, click by click, and experience the consequences directly. This is the core difference between reading a critique and playing one: the argument is not stated, it is enacted through the player's own actions inside the system. This is what Ian Bogost calls procedural rhetoric, meaning persuasion that happens through the rules and processes of a system rather than through images or written text.

The operation of the game reinforces this. The player moves between the four modules, farmland, feedlot, restaurant, and corporate headquarters, making a continuous stream of small operational decisions rather than reading a single fixed argument from start to finish. Because the game has no win condition and does not end, the player is not moving toward a conclusion the way a reader moves toward the end of an article. Instead, the player keeps returning to the same decisions under increasing pressure, which is how the game delivers its message that the system will always demand more, regardless of how it is managed. The message is therefore not something the player reads once, it is something the player comes to understand gradually, through repetition and escalating difficulty, as the same ethical trade-offs keep resurfacing in worse forms.

This is also why the choice of format matters. A PDF or article is finished the moment the reader stops reading it. A game continues to generate new instances of the same argument every time it is played, and because the player is the one making the compromising choices, the critique becomes personal rather than observed from a distance.

Visually, the game uses simple, flat vector graphics, a direct result of the Flash format, which stores images as mathematical instructions rather than pixel data. This kept file sizes small and load times fast on the slower internet connections common at the time. This visual simplicity has been described in scholarship on Molleindustria as deliberate rather than a limitation, keeping player attention on decisions and systems rather than on visual spectacle.

The visual style directly mirrors the tycoon-simulation genre it is critiquing, using the same flat, icon-based, top-down management interface as mainstream business simulation games. This visual familiarity allows the game to appear as ordinary entertainment before its political argument becomes clear through play.

The game also appropriates McDonald's own branding directly, including its color scheme, the Ronald McDonald mascot, and a parody jingle on the main menu. In a version released under the name Burger Tycoon to avoid copyright issues, the mascot was replaced with a bear while the rest of the game remained unchanged. This direct use of real branding is treated by Bogost as central to the anti-advergame genre, since naming and visually referencing a real company is treated as more rhetorically effective than an indirect or fictionalized substitute.
